Alchemist is a client-server web application that scans the records of every grant issued by the NSERC from 1991 to 2014 and categorizes each grant based on subject, field of research, institution, researcher, grant amount, and region. The application then uses this data to project the amount of funding likely to be allocated by the NSERC to a specific subject of interest, field of research or even a specific Professor up to five years into the future.

An analysis on the datasets via the application showed that nearly all fields of research were growing exponentially in both numbers of grants awarded and amount of grants awarded to each field of research. Of the fastest financially growing fields were fields related to health and engineering, while research in humanities, although still exponential, expanded less slowly.

The application was intended for faculty, staff, businesses and students interested in research and the direction in which the a field of research was heading. The app provides other services such as automatic data visualization for each search query and makes more accessible the metrics available within the NSERC grant datasets.

Research

  • RTE-NHSS Proposal
  • UofT RITT Project

The Northview Heights S.S. Department of Chemistry issued a challenge to graduating students to conduct research on how to reduce the school's yearly emissions.

This report presents a closed-loop energy reduction system to the NHSS management with an initial investment cost of under $300,000 projected to self-finance in 16 years and generate an excess of $392,000 in savings over its calculated 40 year lifespan. The design consisted of SPR-327NE-WHT-D sub panel arrays to reduce energy consumption by 17% for the first 10 years and 16% afterwards.

Downloads: Final Report

In 2011, the Water, Sanitation & Hygiene program initiated the RTT Challenge to bring sustainable sanitation solutions to the 2.5 billion people worldwide who do not have access to safe, affordable sanitation.

The University of Toronto team of researchers were assigned a group of student researchers to conduct modulated research to cooperate on the:

  • 1. Reduction of maternal and child mortality and morbidity
  • 2. Scientific and technical solutions for infectious diseases
  • 3. Strengthening India’s scientific translation capacity
  • 4. Scientific and technical advances related to agriculture
  • 5. Scientific advancement in food and nutrition.

As part of a team of five additional student researchers and under the supervision of Professor Joseph Wong, head of political science at University of Toronto, an integrated digestive privy technology and an implementation scheme was compiled as a solution to the problem of sanitation in the global south. After months of research, collaboration and discussion among high school students and their university student mentors, the project resulted in a day-long symposium during which arguments for a potential solution were presented to the global problem of sanitation and poverty. The symposium was held at the Vivian and David Campbell Conference Facility at the Munk School of Global Affairs.
